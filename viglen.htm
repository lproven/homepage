Viglen Genie PCI P5/166

Supplier: Viglen 0181 758 7000

Price: £2,324.10

Verdict: A well-built PC, but Viglen’s cut corners with sound, graphics and hard disk, so not recommended unless you specify the best components yourself.

Viglen is one of the oldest PC builders in Britain, starting out in the Acorn BBC Micro business in the early 80s. More recently, it has been acquired by Amstrad, and now functions as Amstrad’s PC division.

The Genie 166 is assembled from a selection of big-name parts, with only a few minor economies. The heart of the machine is all Intel: a Pentium processor in an Endeavour board. This is the no-frills Endeavour, without sound or graphics - instead it has separate cards: a Matrox Millennium and a SoundBlaster AWE32. The Matrox card is a 2Mb unit, but it uses a 175MHz RAMDAC, limiting it to lower refresh rates than the standard 220MHz version. The AWE32 is also unusual: the normal model has a single IDE port and 2 SIMM sockets for storing extra sound samples. This board has the old-fashioned trinity of proprietary CD-ROM interfaces (Sony, Mitsumi and Panasonic) and has no SIMM sockets for storing extra sound samples. The AWE sounds good on sample playback, but MIDI sounds poor next to Gateway’s Ensoniq.

In terms of build quality, this machine is reasonable. The case is Viglen’s own design, although it’s much like any other mini-tower. The interior is uncluttered, but you need to remove the 3½in drive cage - a simple operation - to access the SIMM slots. The CPU has no fan mounted on it, but there’s one close to it, mounted on the chassis. On the front panel, there’s a bulge which contains activity lights, keyboard lock and reset button. It’s good to see a reset button that needs a pen to trigger, rather than an accidental knock, although it’s odd that there’s a bulge labelled Turbo, but no button. Modern PCs don’t need a turbo button, so why bother to label the gap?

Performance wasn’t great; the Viglen is held back by its Seagate hard disk, and a gigabyte, although a lot, is less than most others supplied. However, the machine is still quick, particularly excelling at playback of AVI and MPEG movies from CD. Sound is delivered through a pair of QuickShot speakers, which are loud, but tinny. The AWE32 sound card is fine for games, but without onboard RAM potential not so good for music enthusiasts. Outside the case is a good, clicky Acer keyboard, and an MS Mouse II. It seems odd to find a company of Amstrad/Viglen’s size and age still using AT keyboards and serial mice, rather than PS/2 equivalents. The monitor is branded Viglen, and is a good, sharp screen, with friendly on-screen menus controlled by intuitive buttons - which include separate ones for brightness and contrast.

Overall, this is a competent but unexciting PC. 166MHz Pentium-class machines are currently top-of-the-line machines, and we don’t expect to see even small cost-cutting measures like this. With a few better components, though, this would be a choice PC.
