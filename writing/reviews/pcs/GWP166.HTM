Gateway 2000 P5/166 Sovereign

Supplier: Gateway 2000 0800 202000

Price: £2,399

Verdict: A powerful and imposing beast, with great expansion potential, for a good price.

As one of the world’s biggest PC manufacturers, it’s rather appropriate that Gateway sent us the biggest PC in this months’ Labs. The Sovereign comes in a newly designed tower case, with a fetching curved fascia. It also has a big keyboard; Gateway’s previous one was a 121-key monster, and the new version has three Windows 95 keys added to it. Although most keyboards now have these keys, you can get by without them; the Start Menu key just simulates Ctrl-Esc, and the "Context Menu" key is similarly Shift-F10.

Inside, the Sovereign is spacious and well laid-out. It has an astonishing twelve drive bays, although filling them all would probably overheat the machine and burn-out its power supply. Most of these occupy the upper part of the case, leaving the motherboard largely unobscured. This is an Intel Endeavour, which follows the new AT-X layout: the CPU is high up on the left, underneath the power supply fan. It’s a minor shame that in such a huge and highly-expandable case, this machine has a comparatively modestly-expandable motherboard; the Endeavour only has three ISA slots, and one of these is shared with a PCI slot to boot.

Gateway mount the serial and parallel ports directly onto the motherboard, too, which further reduces the internal spaghetti. This machine is a pre-production sample; shipping units will have pipeline burst cache as standard, and a larger - but slightly slower - 2Gb hard disk. Also, rough edges like the US set-up in Windows and out-of-date video drivers will be resolved.

The Sovereign comes with two expansion cards as standard: an Ensoniq SoundScape, providing wavetable sound through beefy Altec Lansing speakers, and a Matrox Millennium graphics adapter. Gateway’s Matrox cards use the slower 175MHz RAMDAC as opposed to the full 220MHz version, which means that its cards can’t display their theoretical highest resolutions and refresh rates, but this isn’t much of a loss for most people. Our unit had 4Mb of WRAM installed, but this is an optional extra - the standard configuration is 2Mb. On the software front, you get MS Office Pro and Encarta ’96. The monitor is a pin-sharp 17in Trinitron with easy controls concealed under a small panel.

Gateway skimps on the power supply unit; there’s no power output for the monitor, just a mains-in socket. Turning on this machine involves one switch at the front of the PC, one on the rear of the monitor, and another on the speakers, taking up three mains sockets.

Like most of the larger PC companies, Gateway uses PS/2 keyboards and mice, unlike the smaller builders’ AT keyboards and serial mice. PS/2 mice use IRQ12, which can be a snag in modern interrupt-starved multimedia PCs. In this machine, only one IRQ is free, so you’ll have problems if you want to add both a network card and SCSI adapter.

The Sovereign’s performance comes some way down our list, due mainly to its Western Digital hard disk, but all the faster machines are close anyway - it’s no slouch. Given its huge expansion potential, and the useful bundled MS Office, it’s a good buy.
