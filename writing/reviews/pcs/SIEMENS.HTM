Siemens-Nixdorf Scenic Pro C5/166

Price: £way too much

Build quality: 1/3

Verdict: A company of SNI’s experience should be able to do far better than this. Tragic. Avoid at all costs.

Siemens Nixdorf Informationssysteme, or SNI, are one of Europe’s largest computer manufacturers, selling systems from cash registers to minicomputers. It’s to be expected, then, that their machine will be different from the run-of-the-mill clones, and that it will cost rather more - but we weren’t prepared for this.

The first impression of this PC was formed as we took it out of its box. It’s heavy - especially for a low-profile desktop. The whole chassis is formed from 1mm sheet steel, making it robust and recyclable, but absurdly heavy. Inside is an SNI-designed motherboard, based around the Triton HX chipset and an Intel Pentium. The design, though, is horrendous. The expansion slots are on a riser card, but although this is roughly in the middle of the machine, only one side has slots - wasting the space on the rear, which is obstructed only by a few cables. On the "front" of the riser are four slots - two PCI and only a single shared ISA/PCI, right at the bottom - where it’s fouled by the CPU, voltage regulator, CELP module, and an odd free-standing fan. Only a sub-half-length card would fit. Conversely, the three PCI slots are full-length, despite the scarcity of long PCI cards - and who needs three of them, with on-board graphics and sound? Upgrading the graphics might be popular, as they’re based on an unimpressive Cirrus Logic chipset, accompanied by a SoundBlaster-compatible Vibra16 chip. The sound in and out sockets are handily located on the front panel, but unfortunately the connectors are on the diametrically-opposite corner of the motherboard - so a flylead runs right across the case. The mouse and keyboard connectors really should be here, too.

The riser card is secured by a heavy steel bridge that runs across the centre of the machine. The rear part of this carries the hard disk, which is fair enough - but the front is unused. Considering that the PC has no free drive bays, it’s insane not to put a bay in the front of the bridge as well.

The machine’s performance was poor. Along with the next two slowest systems, from Dell and IBM, it shares a Western Digital hard disk, but SNI has chosen the slower 1.2Gb model rather than the American companies’ 1.6Gb. A 6X Mitsumi FX600S CD rounds out the storage. On the software front, there are no Triton 2 drivers, but there’s a range of preloaded software, including various helpful little utilities, wallpapers etc, and MS Word and Excel, rather than the full Office suite.

SNI has clearly put some effort into this machine’s design, but it has learned nothing from the the competition. The machine is badly designed and ridiculously overpriced.
