Seventeen-inch monitors: how we tested

Feature section

PC Pro's usual performance testing measures the speed at which a computer or component works, but this is obviously meaningless when it comes to monitors, where the main difference is display quality. Flat-panel displays aside, all monitors work in the same way - magnets around the gun steer an electron beam around, while separate circuitry switches the red, green and blue beams on and off. This means that certain types of image are particularly hard to display.

All our tests were performed with Sonera's DisplayMate for Windows, which is possibly the most exhaustive set of monitor tests around. It can generate hundreds of test patterns specifically designed to show up flaws in monitors, and the Windows version can do so at any resolution and colour-depth supported by Windows. We tested at a resolution of 1,024 x 768 pixels, in 65,536 colours, as this is the commonest mode for this size of screen. Whilst some of the monitors and cards can handle the useful 1,152 x 864 resolution, 1,280 x 1,024 is too high for most 17in monitors to cope.

We looked at several different areas:

Basic setup. This includes checking the contrast and brightness controls are correctly set, and examining blank grey and black screens for background interference. A blank screen really should be blank, with no patterns, marks or distortions, and a minimum of reflections.

Geometry and distortion. This covers two main areas; firstly, we looked at the geometry of the screen - with the image size properly adjusted, we checked whether images on screen are accurately represented on the monitor. For instance, squares should be truly square, lines straight, and a ten-pixel gap should be the same length all over the tube. Secondly, we checked regulation. This uses large blinking boxes of different sizes surrounded by a thin line - as the box flashes on and off, on many monitors the line moves, and whilst a millimetre or so is acceptable, more than 3mm is bad. You can try this with a spreadsheet such as Microsoft Excel - hold a sheet of paper up to the screen along one edge, then select the whole sheet: the edge of the display shouldn't move.

Sharpness and resolution. This series of tests looks at the ability of the monitor to display fine detail, using patterns of fine horizontal and vertical lines. We examined both ultra-fine detail and overall focus. Dividing a monitor's dot pitch by its horizontal or vertical size yields the theoretical number of pixels it can display: for instance, the Iiyama Pro's screen is 325mm across, which with a dot pitch of 0.25mm means it should be able to display 1,300 separate pixels across the screen.

Colour and greyscale. Here, we examined colour registration - whether red, green and blue pixels lines up - and streaking and ghosting, as shown by images containing sharp transitions from bright to grey, which are harder to show than bright to dark.
