Panrix Micron P166

Supplier: Panrix 01132-444958

Price: £2,390

Verdict: a well-put-together machine, but nothing outstanding, and Panrix commit the same small sins as others.

Panrix is a familiar name in PC Pro, having won quite a few awards from us in the past for well-built high-performance machines. Today, though, the company’s strategy is changing: than independently choosing all the parts and assembling its own machines, it’s entered a partnership with respected US manufacturer Micron. Essentially, Panrix are assembling the same machines as Micron sells in America, and selling them here under their own name as the Micron range.

The case is the same as ever, and still says Panrix on the front. It’s a good design - this variant is the mini-tower, and it’s also used by Armari and Polar this month. The lid is removed easily after undoing a single thumbscrew at the rear, but after that, the case removes easily. With three drives fitted, there are still three free bays, which is quite reasonable. What is neat is the rear hard disk cage, which is mounted over the PSU. It’s on a hinge, which makes fitting or inspecting hard disks easy. All the drive bays are at the top of the tower, leaving the motherboard largely unobscured. The CPU is at the bottom, fouling any long PCI cards, but we haven’t seen any of these, so it’s not critical. There are only six slots in total, which is a bit stingy, but none are shared, which is a plus. There’s a dummy slot right at the bottom, where a small daughter board connects to supply sound connectors to the rear panel. These connect to the onboard Vibra16 sound chip, which offers reasonable 16-bit SoundBlaster-compatible sound. Sample playback is reasonable, but there’s a noticeable hiss in the background. However, playback of audio CDs sounds excellent through the brilliant Yamaha speakers.

Other components are good, if unsurprising, choices: a 1.2Gb Quantum Fireball and a Teac six-speed CD-ROM drive. A Supra V.34 modem is a pleasing extra, sensibly configured to interrupt 11 to avoid conflicts with the MS Mouse on COM1. The display is handled by a Matrox MGA Millennium with a 220MHz DAC, outputting to a bright and crisp Idek Iiyama VisionMaster 17 - a good screen, albeit with fiddly on-screen menus: everything, even brightness and contrast, is controlled by just three buttons.

As far as the Panrix’s software set-up goes, it’s like the curate’s egg: good in parts. It’s commendable to see that Panrix has installed DOS drivers for the sound card and CD-ROM, but the monitor was left set as "Unknown", and none of the extras such as CompCore’s software MPEG decoder were installed. Other minor glitches were the spurious PS/2 mouse port detected by Windows 95 - once a common problem, but getting less common now with newer BIOSs. Panrix offer two good value Microsoft application bundles: either Office Professional (with Access 95) for the business user, or a 14-CD bundle of SoHo/Leisure packages, including Works, Money, Encarta and many others.

Overall, though, this machine is a little disappointing. Though nothing’s obviously wrong, the performance is some way behind the leading machines’ and the software setup isn’t as thorough as it could, and should, be.
