Office Techniques T166+

Price: £1,400

Supplier: 01923-211449

Build quality: 3/3

Verdict: A solid, if unexciting, machine, but very basic software setup.

At first glance, inside and out, the resemblance between Office Techniques T166+ and West One’s Vivace is striking. Both use the same screwless minitower case, Cyrix 6x86 CPUs on Tyan motherboards, Quantum Fireball 1280 hard disks and STB Lightspeed 128 graphics cards. However, there are several differences; the T166+ is built around the Triton VX chipset, rather than the HX, and has two DIMM banks for SDRAM modules. This is still scarce, though, so Office Techniques supplied the machine EDO SIMMs instead. In tests, the performance gains from SDRAM are negligible anyway - see the boxout on pXXX.

The VX Tyan has one less ISA slot than the HX version, and Office Techniques has used the spare opening in the backplane to mount a PS/2 mouse port. Whilst it’s good to see PS/2 mice, we prefer all the ports in case cutouts. Other minor flaws are the location of the hard disk and some of the cables that make working in the machine more awkward. To keep the drive cables tidy, they’re held in place with a metal clip that’s stuck onto the bottom of the hard disk, right on one of the drive controller chips. This brings the metal perilously close to the legs of the chips, where it could potentially cause a short-circuit that could damage the machine, though this is pretty unlikely. A neat touch is the extra-long CD audio lead that’s routed right around the edge of the case, rather than straight across the motherboard like most of the other systems.

The sound card is a full SoundBlaster AWE32, with all the optional software installed, including Creative Labs’ impressive new Windows 95 drivers. The IDE port can’t be disabled, but it’s been reconfigured to IRQ 10 so as not to conflict with the onboard EIDE, and the controller is labelled as "Creative Labs IDE" in the control panel - neat. We also liked the small supporting brace stuck onto the case that holds the end of the ¾ length card firmly. It drives a pair of Soundwave 240 speakers, which have a good, solid sound, but not as impressive as Yamaha’s. The only other card is the STB Lightspeed, based on Tseng Labs’ 128-bit ET6000 chip. It’s a fast card, but lacks 3D acceleration. The clear, steady display is a Samsung SyncMaster 15Gli.

Overall, this is a competent, well-built machine, with a range of good, solid parts. However, no great effort has been lavished upon the software setup aside from installing the necessary device drivers - no Triton 2 or busmastering EIDE drivers, no extras or preinstalled software. There’s nothing major wrong with it, but there’s little to especially commend it, either.
